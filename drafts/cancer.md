### üì¶ Meet Robert, 68 (Retired Accountant & Family Cancer Survivor)

**Before:**
Robert retired at 66. Active, healthy, no major health issues. Evening routine: two beers most nights, sometimes three on weekends. About 14-18 drinks per week. "Moderate," by any standard. His father died of colorectal cancer at 72. His wife, Linda, was diagnosed with breast cancer at 62 (now in remission, doing well).

After Linda's diagnosis, Robert read everything he could about cancer prevention. He knew about smoking (never smoked), processed meat (cut back), exercise (walked daily). **The Data:**
Robert's drinking pattern (2 drinks/day, 14-18/week) falls squarely in the "moderate" range that still raises cancer risk. Bagnardi (2015) shows:
- Colorectal cancer (men): RR 1.07 per 10 g/day. At 28 g/day, Robert's risk is elevated ~20% compared to non-drinkers.

- Even at "moderate" levels, cumulative exposure over decades matters.

Linda's breast cancer: alcohol is one of the few modifiable risk factors. Even 1 drink/day raises breast cancer risk ~5% per 10 g/day (Bagnardi 2015). Linda had been drinking wine daily for years before diagnosis.

## The Successful Aging Angle

Cancer prevention is one of the highest-leverage health investments you can make after 60. You can't control all cancer risk, but you **can** control this one‚Äîand the payoff is enormous. Every alcohol-free year reduces cumulative exposure to a Group 1 carcinogen across multiple organ systems. This isn't about fear; it's about **buying more healthy years** to spend with people you love, doing things that matter. The choice is clear: alcohol offers zero benefit, and cutting it out stacks the deck in your favor ([RoweKahn1997] successful aging: low disease risk is modifiable, and prevention compounds over time).
